The Renaissance



Have you ever wondered how our modern society came to be? Well it does not really matter, you have still arrived at the perfect website. The Renaissance began in Italy and rapidly spread throughout Europe in the 16th century, an era of many reforming eye-opening new discoveries. The epoch ranged from the 14th-17th century and is characterized by profound revolutionary reforms in societal norms, morals and values. In short these are the main incidents and cultural currents that evolved during this period.

  • The Reformation
  • Humanism
  • Secularism
  • Poetic pioneering


It all started when people criticized the restricted and hypocritical catholic church. The German monk Martin Luther, took advantage of the new art of printing to help publish and disperse the 95 theses, and also stated that wealthier individuals should not have the possibility to buy themselves a “ticket to heaven” through the sales of indulgences. This incident came to be known as the Reformation and it started a new mindset which did not allow belief and religion to have an influence on politics, also called secularism.

During the Renaissance, authors and leaders focused more on the individual as the centre of attention, instead of God and the church, when writing. The classical Greek and Roman art and thought was rediscovered and as time went on, human life and right was emphasised. This also popularised new poetic forms, such as the sonnet, which is a fourteen-line poem.

Furthermore this period was signified by the shift of paradigms that permeated in science, religion, economy, culture, legislation and society.




Main Authors and Their Work


Christopher Marlowe

Christopher Marlowe was a poet and a playwright at the end of the Renaissance in the 16th century. His work influenced several printers such as William Shakespeare and others. Marlowe was born in 1564 in Canterbury, England and died on February 26, 1593, as a 29-year-old when he was murdered.


Unfortunately, Marlowe's literary career lasted less than 6 years. Nevertheless, he was one of the first English dramatists to show the full potential of using dramatic ballads, as well as the first one to use the miserable consequences of Humanism in the Renaissance.


It is believed that the wrote between 4 and 7 works and one of the most famous work is "The Jew of Malta", "Edward the Second" and "The Massacre at Paris".


John Donne

John Donne, is considered to be the greatest English love poet. He was born in 1572 in London, England and died on March 31, 1631, in London. Donne is known as a founder of the metaphysical poets, and he is well noted for his strong faith.


Only seven poems were published during his life and among them "The Dream", "The Flea" and "The Sun Rising" are the most famous.


John Milton

John Milton was born on December 9, 1608, in London, England and died when he turned 65 on November 8, 1674, in London. He is considered to be the most famous English writer and poet after William Shakespeare and has several great works.


His most famous work is “Paradise Lost”, “Paradise Regained” and “Samson Agonistes”.

Analysis of Shakespeare's Hamlet


Hamlet is a play written by Shakespeare at the end of the renaissance. Hamlet is one of his most famous works and it has been performed since its release in 1609. The play is about love, murder, revenge and moral within the Danish royalty. The King of Denmark is murdered by his brother, the son of the dead King then returns from England to get revenge.

Shakespeare has a debate about moral in the play while creating an incredible story that the audience loves. He tries to show us how punishment always come for the sinners but also that revenge may not be the way to punish thus you have now sinned yourself. At the end of the play all the main characters dies, including the prince who came for revenge.

Hamlet was written during a time in England where the King or Queen had all the power and their word was law. This means you could not express all opinions of the monarch, yet Shakespeare wrote this play about infidelity and murder using another monarch family. This could have been a way for him to create the idea that the rulers are not perfect, that they are just people.

To me, humanism influenced Shakespeare’s work quite a lot. In Hamlet an example of that would be the way he writes about strong human emotions and puts them in the center of the play. He creates all these characters with different relationships to each other as well as different hardships. For example, the son who comes for revenge is in love with a girl, his goal is to kill his father's murderer, which would be his lovers father. There are so many strong feelings in the play, they are exaggerated but in a way very relatable for many. This is why William Shakespeare and his work has lived for so long and will live forever.
